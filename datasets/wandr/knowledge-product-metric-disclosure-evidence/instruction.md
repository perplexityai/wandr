## Universal rules

The following rules apply to every task below.

**Identifier discipline.** Same entity → same string. Different entities → different strings. When you're unsure whether two names refer to the same thing (spelling variants, model editions, product versions), don't offload the ambiguity to the user — decide and commit. Don't hedge by splitting "just in case" or merging "probably close enough." Both failures cost credit.

**More is (usually) better.** Whenever the task says "at least N" / "N+" / etc, going past N generally helps your score — treat those as soft floors, not exact targets.

**No duplicate entities.** Do not, however increase volume via duplicate entities, all the entities must be meaningfully different, entity-duplicating rows will generally be penalized; in particular, do not supply multiple rows per entity to "supply the answer in chunks", which will also be treated as entity duplication.

**Every `url` you submit must be fetchable.** Do not submit URLs you expect to be non-resolvable (DNS failure, dead host) as your `url`. Evidence should come from an available `url` (even if you wished to, say, provide evidence for some URL's unhealthiness).

**Every row carries `excerpts`** — verbatim or near-verbatim quotes from the source page (whitespace, punctuation, ellipses to skip irrelevant clauses are fine) **with semantics preserved**. An excerpt is what the page literally says, in the meaning the page intends. Fabrication, paraphrase that shifts meaning, sentence-stitching across sections, or selective cropping that flips a hedge into confidence — all fail.

The excerpts collectively make the answer evident. *Every* task-required claim / task-asked question / answer field / etc MUST have its support visible somewhere in the excerpt set — not just nearby on the page. The reader's test: imagine someone sees only your excerpts (with no access to the rest of the page); can they verify each piece of your answer? If a page genuinely doesn't carry what the task asks for, find a different page or skip the entity rather than fish for tangential excerpts. If you deem paraphrasing necessary / desirable for proper answer delivery, that's admirable and encouraged: paraphrase to your heart's desire within `answer` fields, make new `answer` fields and redistribute summaries among them as you see fit, but excerpts stay faithful and fully evidence-complete.

**Page contents only.** This is a task about citing web pages for human consumers, and citations are expected to be human-usable — both in where they are sourced from and in how well they stand on their own, out of page context. Excerpts come from the web-page main text — what a human reader sees on the page. Excerpts should also look sensible by themselves, with their information-bearing intent clear. API response blobs, page metadata fields (timestamps, view counts, score numbers), structured-data payloads (`__NEXT_DATA__`, JSON-LD, OpenGraph), and other “robot-side” sources / page representations are out of scope. In a similar vein, be wary of citing image captions / on-hover alt text / infoboxes / specially rendered bibliography or reference units / UI or navigation elements / etc. (unless confident in both their visibility and critical utility for the task), and avoid citing image contents, hyperlink-encoded URLs, and similar evidence surfaces altogether: anything outside the straightforward “main body of text” risks reducing citation ergonomics to the point where it is considered unusable.

**Signaling absence.** If you mean for a blank or sentinel `answer` field to assert "this required information isn't on the page" (vs. "I missed it"): first verify the task warrants such an option — many tasks treat blank-required as an invalid entity. When absence IS admitted, flag the intent explicitly in an appropriately-named `answer` field, and let your excerpts carry the strongest available evidence — direct proof-of-absence ("not listed", "n/a") if the page provides it; otherwise, try at least capturing the page segments where the missing info would plausibly have appeared if it existed, where applicable.

## `knowledge_product_metric_disclosure_evidence`

For 150+ organization/product pairs where the named organization visibly owns, maintains, publishes, or directly stands behind a public knowledge product, curated data product, database, dataset, benchmark, registry, documentation/data platform, evidence resource, or comparable corpus-bearing product, cover each of the 3 metric disclosure facets listed below by supplying a source-stated, product-scoped metric disclosure from a facet-appropriate public disclosure role (i.e. 1+ URL under each facet).

The task captures public provenance for product-scoped metric disclosures. Each accepted URL or visibly cited section must provide load-bearing evidence for the exact organization/product/facet relationship you submit. The metric only needs to be stated by the page; the source does not need to prove that the metric is true, adequate, current beyond the fetched page, or useful for legal, medical, financial, procurement, or buying decisions.

The submitted product must be the product-level knowledge or data asset, not a routine item inside that asset. Do not submit individual database records, accession pages, protein/gene/chemical/entity pages, article records, catalog rows, source-list entries, connector/integration rows, benchmark questions, issue pages, or other per-item pages as separate products merely because they carry counts, statuses, revision history, reviewed flags, annotation scores, or other standard record metadata. Such pages can support a valid product only when the named submitted product is the maintained database, dataset, benchmark, registry, corpus, or product-level resource itself and the cited passage binds the metric to that product-level asset.

Do not fan out one umbrella product family into many easy leaves from a single shared comparative stats page, dashboard, index, catalog table, or family-wide scorecard. A submitted `organization_product` should be a distinct product-level knowledge/data asset, not merely a locale, language edition, market edition, regional edition, version, instance, mirror, sibling dataset, record family, or subproduct variant inside one umbrella family when the only evidence is a shared family surface. A family-wide table can support the umbrella product when the metric is scoped to that umbrella product, but it does not validate many sibling variants as separate products unless the cited page provides separately maintained variant-level product framing and the facet metric is specifically scoped to that submitted variant.

Metric disclosure facets:
- `coverage_scope`: concrete coverage, scale, completeness, scope, or corpus-size disclosure, such as counts of records, cases, works, securities, jurisdictions, topics, structures, documents, features, tasks, pages, sources, languages, or explicit coverage/completeness percentages. Generic article-index context, registry metadata, catalog table columns, and broad "comprehensive resource" language are not enough without a product-scoped count or scope statement.
- `freshness_update`: product-level release, changelog, update-history, currentness-policy, dated data-publication/release-cycle, maintained-update mechanism, data-latency, versioning, or addition-rate disclosure tied to ongoing product maintenance. A one-time donated, released, created, launched, or published date is not enough by itself. Quick-facts periodicity, generic program cadence, broad ongoing-maintenance language, a table/dashboard auto-refresh cadence, page revision timestamp, update badge, citation date, registry `modified` timestamp, or update column in an inventory only counts when the cited passage describes a product-level release/currentness mechanism or dated publication cycle for the submitted product itself, not merely freshness of the disclosure page, program overview, or comparison table.
- `quality_assurance`: concrete product-specific methodology, review, curation, validation, evidence-grading, accuracy/error/uncertainty, benchmark/evaluation, analyst/editorial review, data-quality-control, audit, provenance checking, review status, or source-traceability disclosure with a concrete process or measured outcome for the named product. A stated process or named grading scheme can count when numeric quality metrics are not native to the product, but it must be product-scoped and substantive; a shallow catalog attribute such as a missing-values flag, file type, task type, license, format field, publisher/source column, registry metadata field, generic abstract keyword, or generic import/process/combine statement is not enough by itself. Generic claims that a product is curated, reviewed, quality controlled, combined, imported, methodological, authoritative, trusted, or provenance-aware are not enough without concrete product-specific process detail or measured quality/evaluation evidence. A descriptive proxy statistic, engagement/usage/coverage-derived score, article-depth-style score, generic health/completeness indicator, or popularity/activity metric is not quality assurance unless the page ties it to an explicit quality-control, review, validation, curation, evaluation, audit, or error-checking methodology for the submitted product.

The page should function as a public disclosure surface through ownership, maintainer/authorship, canonical project context, product docs/report framing, methodology framing, official stats or dashboard context, dataset/benchmark paper context, registry-record framing, or direct non-advisory attribution of the stated metric. The same URL can support multiple facets only when it contains clearly separable product-scoped passages or sections that visibly play the relevant disclosure roles, such as coverage/statistics, release/currentness, and methodology/QA. A generic official about page, handbook overview, catalog card, product homepage, dashboard, broad methodology page, family-wide stats page, comparative dashboard, broad scorecard, many-source catalog, source list, connector directory, integration index, article index, dataset registry, broad product list, or upstream-source inventory can work only when the visible page, record, row, paper passage, or section binds the facet-specific metric to the submitted organization's own product rather than merely listing a sibling variant, locale, edition, source, item, input, or broad program overview. Third-party listicles, vendor rankings, buyer guides, generic review pages, aggregators, ordinary company metrics, vague marketing adjectives, and missing-state or source-bookkeeping labels are not metric disclosures.

Requirements:
- The page must clearly identify the named organization/product or product-maintainer relationship; inclusion in an aggregator's catalog, article index, dataset registry, source list, connector table, integration index, or upstream-source inventory is not enough when the visible publisher or maintainer is someone else.
- The page must identify the product as a knowledge/data product, not just a generic SaaS app, company, blog, vendor, or service.
- The submitted product must be a product-level knowledge/data asset. An individual record, accession, entity page, article, row, source entry, connector, benchmark item, or comparable per-item page inside a larger product is not a valid product, even if the page has its own status, version history, reviewed flag, length/count, annotation score, or metadata fields. Likewise, a language/locale/edition/market/version/instance/mirror/subproduct variant inside one product family is not valid when the cited evidence is only a shared comparative table, dashboard, index, catalog, or scorecard; such a source can support the umbrella product, but a sibling variant needs separately maintained variant-level product framing plus a metric specifically scoped to that variant.
- The page should make its facet-specific public disclosure role visible through page context, ownership, maintainer/authorship, canonical project framing, product docs/report framing, methodology framing, official stats/dashboard context, dataset/benchmark paper context, registry-record framing, or direct attribution of the stated product metric: for `coverage_scope`, a coverage, statistics, data-profile, corpus description, scoped dashboard, product report, or analogous section/passage; for `freshness_update`, a product-level release note, changelog, update history, currentness policy, data-publication schedule, release cycle, maintained-update mechanism, latency statement, versioning passage, or analogous currentness section/passage; for `quality_assurance`, a methodology, validation, curation, evaluation, error/uncertainty, audit, provenance/source-traceability, review-status, quality-control, or analogous QA section/passage. A generic about page, handbook overview, catalog card, product homepage, dashboard, broad methodology page, or portal description does not satisfy multiple facets unless it has clearly separable product-scoped passages or sections supporting each selected facet.

- The page must make the metric's scope visible enough to know what is being counted, actively updated, refreshed, monitored, versioned, graded, reviewed, validated, checked, benchmarked, completed, or otherwise measured for the submitted product.
