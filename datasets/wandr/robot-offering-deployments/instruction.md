## Universal rules

The following rules apply to every task below.

**Identifier discipline.** Same entity → same string. Different entities → different strings. When you're unsure whether two names refer to the same thing (spelling variants, model editions, product versions), don't offload the ambiguity to the user — decide and commit. Don't hedge by splitting "just in case" or merging "probably close enough." Both failures cost credit.

**More is (usually) better.** Whenever the task says "at least N" / "N+" / etc, going past N generally helps your score — treat those as soft floors, not exact targets.

**No duplicate entities.** Do not, however increase volume via duplicate entities, all the entities must be meaningfully different, entity-duplicating rows will generally be penalized; in particular, do not supply multiple rows per entity to "supply the answer in chunks", which will also be treated as entity duplication.

**Every `url` you submit must be fetchable.** Do not submit URLs you expect to be non-resolvable (DNS failure, dead host) as your `url`. Evidence should come from an available `url` (even if you wished to, say, provide evidence for some URL's unhealthiness).

**Every row carries `excerpts`** — verbatim or near-verbatim quotes from the source page (whitespace, punctuation, ellipses to skip irrelevant clauses are fine) **with semantics preserved**. An excerpt is what the page literally says, in the meaning the page intends. Fabrication, paraphrase that shifts meaning, sentence-stitching across sections, or selective cropping that flips a hedge into confidence — all fail.

The excerpts collectively make the answer evident. *Every* task-required claim / task-asked question / answer field / etc MUST have its support visible somewhere in the excerpt set — not just nearby on the page. The reader's test: imagine someone sees only your excerpts (with no access to the rest of the page); can they verify each piece of your answer? If a page genuinely doesn't carry what the task asks for, find a different page or skip the entity rather than fish for tangential excerpts. If you deem paraphrasing necessary / desirable for proper answer delivery, that's admirable and encouraged: paraphrase to your heart's desire within `answer` fields, make new `answer` fields and redistribute summaries among them as you see fit, but excerpts stay faithful and fully evidence-complete.

**Page contents only.** This is a task about citing web pages for human consumers, and citations are expected to be human-usable — both in where they are sourced from and in how well they stand on their own, out of page context. Excerpts come from the web-page main text — what a human reader sees on the page. Excerpts should also look sensible by themselves, with their information-bearing intent clear. API response blobs, page metadata fields (timestamps, view counts, score numbers), structured-data payloads (`__NEXT_DATA__`, JSON-LD, OpenGraph), and other “robot-side” sources / page representations are out of scope. In a similar vein, be wary of citing image captions / on-hover alt text / infoboxes / specially rendered bibliography or reference units / UI or navigation elements / etc. (unless confident in both their visibility and critical utility for the task), and avoid citing image contents, hyperlink-encoded URLs, and similar evidence surfaces altogether: anything outside the straightforward “main body of text” risks reducing citation ergonomics to the point where it is considered unusable.

**Signaling absence.** If you mean for a blank or sentinel `answer` field to assert "this required information isn't on the page" (vs. "I missed it"): first verify the task warrants such an option — many tasks treat blank-required as an invalid entity. When absence IS admitted, flag the intent explicitly in an appropriately-named `answer` field, and let your excerpts carry the strongest available evidence — direct proof-of-absence ("not listed", "n/a") if the page provides it; otherwise, try at least capturing the page segments where the missing info would plausibly have appeared if it existed, where applicable.

## `robot_offering_deployments`

For 150+ companies that supply industrial, mobile, warehouse, logistics, manufacturing, inspection, or facility-operations robot systems or robot-operations platforms, name 1+ robot offering per company; for each company/offering pair and each of the 2 proof sides below, supply a source (i.e. 1+ URL).

Official product pages make robotics catalogs easy; the harder part is connecting the same named offering to a public operating setting without treating deployment stories as substitutes for offering-presentation pages.

The proof sides of interest, which we refer to as `proof_side`, are:
- `official_offering`: the product owner or company presents the named robot offering, product line, robot family, robotic system, or robot-operations platform on an offering, product-family, portfolio, catalog, documentation, or product-announcement surface whose primary role is to present the offering as an offering.
- `deployment_proof`: a public operating setting presents the offering, product line, or sufficiently specific company robot family in deployment, installation, pilot, roll-out, case-study, customer, operator, agency, site, or project context.

`company` ought to be a real supplier of embodied robot systems or robot-fleet/control platforms tied to physical robots in the listed industrial, mobile, warehouse, logistics, manufacturing, inspection, or facility-operations contexts. Generic automation consultancies, pure system integrators, distributors, resellers, generic AI/cloud/software vendors, and conventional material-handling vendors do not count unless they have a repeatable named robot product, robot line, robotic system, or robot-operations platform. `company_robot_offering` ought to be a named robot product, robot family, robotic system, AMR/AGV line, cobot or industrial-arm line, mobile-manipulation system, goods-to-person or AS/RS robot system, inspection robot, facility robot, or robotics fleet/control platform coupled to physical robot operations. Product families and platforms count when marketed as offerings; arbitrary model variants should not be split off unless public pages present them as distinct named offerings. Robot-specific software counts only when directly tied to operating, orchestrating, monitoring, controlling, simulating, or deploying physical robots. Pure components such as grippers, sensors, cameras, motors, batteries, controllers, and safety devices do not count unless bundled and marketed as a robot system or robot offering. Broad directories, thin exhibitor pages, award-only pages, generic product/application prose, customer logo walls, anonymous customer claims, generic WMS/ERP/analytics/software pages, lab-only concepts, and speculative future MoUs do not count by themselves. The sources should be public, accessible, and usable.

Requirements:
- The page must clearly identify the claimed company or product owner and the claimed robot offering, product line, platform, or sufficiently specific robot family.
- The page must show that the offering is an embodied robot system or robot-specific operations platform for industrial, mobile, warehouse, logistics, manufacturing, inspection, or facility-operations work.
- The page should make its proof-side source role visible: for `official_offering`, product-owner or company-controlled standing on an offering/product/product-family/product-owner presentation surface, not a deployment, case-study, customer, operator, site, or project page merely because that page is company-controlled and names the product; for `deployment_proof`, deployment-specific case, customer, site, operator, agency, project, installation, pilot, roll-out, public filing, reputable trade article, or comparable operating-setting treatment.
- The page must contribute proof-side substance: for `official_offering`, it presents the named offering as a marketed robot product, product line, platform, or system in an offering-presentation role; for `deployment_proof`, it identifies a concrete customer, site, facility, operator, agency, project, installation, pilot, roll-out, or comparable operating setting and ties it to the claimed offering, product line, or sufficiently specific company robot family.
