## Universal rules

The following rules apply to every task below.

**Identifier discipline.** Same entity → same string. Different entities → different strings. When you're unsure whether two names refer to the same thing (spelling variants, model editions, product versions), don't offload the ambiguity to the user — decide and commit. Don't hedge by splitting "just in case" or merging "probably close enough." Both failures cost credit.

**More is (usually) better.** Whenever the task says "at least N" / "N+" / etc, going past N generally helps your score — treat those as soft floors, not exact targets.

**No duplicate entities.** Do not, however increase volume via duplicate entities, all the entities must be meaningfully different, entity-duplicating rows will generally be penalized; in particular, do not supply multiple rows per entity to "supply the answer in chunks", which will also be treated as entity duplication.

**Every `url` you submit must be fetchable.** Do not submit URLs you expect to be non-resolvable (DNS failure, dead host) as your `url`. Evidence should come from an available `url` (even if you wished to, say, provide evidence for some URL's unhealthiness).

**Every row carries `excerpts`** — verbatim or near-verbatim quotes from the source page (whitespace, punctuation, ellipses to skip irrelevant clauses are fine) **with semantics preserved**. An excerpt is what the page literally says, in the meaning the page intends. Fabrication, paraphrase that shifts meaning, sentence-stitching across sections, or selective cropping that flips a hedge into confidence — all fail.

The excerpts collectively make the answer evident. *Every* task-required claim / task-asked question / answer field / etc MUST have its support visible somewhere in the excerpt set — not just nearby on the page. The reader's test: imagine someone sees only your excerpts (with no access to the rest of the page); can they verify each piece of your answer? If a page genuinely doesn't carry what the task asks for, find a different page or skip the entity rather than fish for tangential excerpts. If you deem paraphrasing necessary / desirable for proper answer delivery, that's admirable and encouraged: paraphrase to your heart's desire within `answer` fields, make new `answer` fields and redistribute summaries among them as you see fit, but excerpts stay faithful and fully evidence-complete.

**Page contents only.** This is a task about citing web pages for human consumers, and citations are expected to be human-usable — both in where they are sourced from and in how well they stand on their own, out of page context. Excerpts come from the web-page main text — what a human reader sees on the page. Excerpts should also look sensible by themselves, with their information-bearing intent clear. API response blobs, page metadata fields (timestamps, view counts, score numbers), structured-data payloads (`__NEXT_DATA__`, JSON-LD, OpenGraph), and other “robot-side” sources / page representations are out of scope. In a similar vein, be wary of citing image captions / on-hover alt text / infoboxes / specially rendered bibliography or reference units / UI or navigation elements / etc. (unless confident in both their visibility and critical utility for the task), and avoid citing image contents, hyperlink-encoded URLs, and similar evidence surfaces altogether: anything outside the straightforward “main body of text” risks reducing citation ergonomics to the point where it is considered unusable.

**Signaling absence.** If you mean for a blank or sentinel `answer` field to assert "this required information isn't on the page" (vs. "I missed it"): first verify the task warrants such an option — many tasks treat blank-required as an invalid entity. When absence IS admitted, flag the intent explicitly in an appropriately-named `answer` field, and let your excerpts carry the strongest available evidence — direct proof-of-absence ("not listed", "n/a") if the page provides it; otherwise, try at least capturing the page segments where the missing info would plausibly have appeared if it existed, where applicable.

## `industrial_automation_public_presence_evidence`

Identify 120+ companies as industrial-automation, industrial-distribution, systems-integration, motion-control, pneumatics, robotics, controls, sensors/vision, or manufacturing-technology businesses; for each company, cover each of the 4 public-presence facets and each of the 2 source roles listed below with a facet-centered source that exposes a focused, tangible company-scoped finding (i.e. 1+ URL under each facet and source role).

The purpose is to build a broad public-presence evidence map for industrial automation companies rather than a competitor ranking, website audit, or outreach list.

Presence facets:
- `owned_offering`: evidence of a concrete industrial automation offering, capability, product family, service, integration, application, or solution offered by or attributed to the company
- `manufacturer_relationship`: evidence of a public relationship between the company and a manufacturer, technology brand, or product line
- `served_market_or_geography`: evidence of a concrete operating footprint, branch/facility/lab/fulfillment location, service territory, registry or association footprint, or named customer/industry/geographic market served
- `public_activity_signal`: evidence of concrete public activity by or about the company beyond static general identity, such as an event, training, hiring, case/project/application, news/acquisition, launch, or comparable activity anchor

Source roles:
- `company_attributed`: facet-centered evidence surfaced through the company's own or visibly company-attributed voice
- `external_context`: facet-centered evidence surfaced through a distinct outside party, counterparty, organizer, publisher, marketplace, registry, or comparable non-company context

Companies ought to be real public businesses in the industrial automation ecosystem; generic IT firms, unrelated B2B services, generic manufacturers with no automation/distribution/integration relevance, source publishers, and product categories or product lines submitted as companies are out of scope. Sources should be public, accessible, usable, and centered enough on the selected facet and role for the page itself to carry the evidence. General encyclopedia pages, broad company-profile/database pages, scraped contact or lead-list profiles, and generic home/about pages usually lack enough task-local context unless the page structure is materially centered on the selected facet and role.

Requirements:
- The page must clearly identify the named company as a substantive subject, source owner, listed distributor/integrator, employer, event participant, case-study actor, customer/market actor, or activity subject rather than only an incidental peer in a list.
- The page should make the selected source role visible and facet-centered: for `company_attributed`, it should communicate a company-controlled, company-authored, official, or otherwise company-attributed channel carrying the selected facet; for `external_context`, it should communicate a distinct non-company page context such as a manufacturer, technology brand, customer, event organizer, trade/news publisher, association, registry, marketplace, job board, or comparable outside surface carrying facet-specific company-scoped evidence. A general encyclopedia article, broad company profile, scraped directory, contact database, lead list, or generic outside overview is not enough merely because it is outside the company's own site.
- The page should make its facet-appropriate context visible. For `owned_offering`, this means a source context centered on a specific industrial automation product family, catalog line, technology, service, integration, application, or solution offered by or attributed to the company, not just a general statement that the company provides automation. For `manufacturer_relationship`, this means a distributor, supplier, partner, locator, line-card, where-to-buy, product-line, integration, or comparable relationship context naming both the company and a manufacturer, technology brand, or product line. For `served_market_or_geography`, this means an industries-served/customer-market page, branch/location/facility/lab/fulfillment page, service-territory page, association or registry member record, customer/market case, or comparable operating-scope context; a lone headquarters address or generic state/country line in a broad profile is too thin. For `public_activity_signal`, this means a concrete activity surface such as a dated trade-show or exhibitor page, training or event page, case study, job posting, acquisition or news item, launch, applied-solution page, or comparable public activity beyond generic homepage copy.
- The page should expose a focused finding clearly scoped to the named company and selected facet. For `owned_offering`, this means a named offering detail such as a product family, catalog area, automation technology, service capability, integration, application, or solution. For `manufacturer_relationship`, this means a concrete named relationship detail involving the company and the manufacturer/brand/product line. For `served_market_or_geography`, this means a specific served market, customer segment, branch, facility/lab/fulfillment location, service territory, geography served, or association/registry footprint, not a headquarters line alone. For `public_activity_signal`, this means a concrete public activity detail such as event participation, training topic/date, hiring role, case-study application, acquisition/news fact, launch, or applied-solution activity.
