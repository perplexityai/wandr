## Universal rules

The following rules apply to every task below.

**Identifier discipline.** Same entity → same string. Different entities → different strings. When you're unsure whether two names refer to the same thing (spelling variants, model editions, product versions), don't offload the ambiguity to the user — decide and commit. Don't hedge by splitting "just in case" or merging "probably close enough." Both failures cost credit.

**More is (usually) better.** Whenever the task says "at least N" / "N+" / etc, going past N generally helps your score — treat those as soft floors, not exact targets.

**No duplicate entities.** Do not, however increase volume via duplicate entities, all the entities must be meaningfully different, entity-duplicating rows will generally be penalized; in particular, do not supply multiple rows per entity to "supply the answer in chunks", which will also be treated as entity duplication.

**Every `url` you submit must be fetchable.** Do not submit URLs you expect to be non-resolvable (DNS failure, dead host) as your `url`. Evidence should come from an available `url` (even if you wished to, say, provide evidence for some URL's unhealthiness).

**Every row carries `excerpts`** — verbatim or near-verbatim quotes from the source page (whitespace, punctuation, ellipses to skip irrelevant clauses are fine) **with semantics preserved**. An excerpt is what the page literally says, in the meaning the page intends. Fabrication, paraphrase that shifts meaning, sentence-stitching across sections, or selective cropping that flips a hedge into confidence — all fail.

The excerpts collectively make the answer evident. *Every* task-required claim / task-asked question / answer field / etc MUST have its support visible somewhere in the excerpt set — not just nearby on the page. The reader's test: imagine someone sees only your excerpts (with no access to the rest of the page); can they verify each piece of your answer? If a page genuinely doesn't carry what the task asks for, find a different page or skip the entity rather than fish for tangential excerpts. If you deem paraphrasing necessary / desirable for proper answer delivery, that's admirable and encouraged: paraphrase to your heart's desire within `answer` fields, make new `answer` fields and redistribute summaries among them as you see fit, but excerpts stay faithful and fully evidence-complete.

**Page contents only.** This is a task about citing web pages for human consumers, and citations are expected to be human-usable — both in where they are sourced from and in how well they stand on their own, out of page context. Excerpts come from the web-page main text — what a human reader sees on the page. Excerpts should also look sensible by themselves, with their information-bearing intent clear. API response blobs, page metadata fields (timestamps, view counts, score numbers), structured-data payloads (`__NEXT_DATA__`, JSON-LD, OpenGraph), and other “robot-side” sources / page representations are out of scope. In a similar vein, be wary of citing image captions / on-hover alt text / infoboxes / specially rendered bibliography or reference units / UI or navigation elements / etc. (unless confident in both their visibility and critical utility for the task), and avoid citing image contents, hyperlink-encoded URLs, and similar evidence surfaces altogether: anything outside the straightforward “main body of text” risks reducing citation ergonomics to the point where it is considered unusable.

**Signaling absence.** If you mean for a blank or sentinel `answer` field to assert "this required information isn't on the page" (vs. "I missed it"): first verify the task warrants such an option — many tasks treat blank-required as an invalid entity. When absence IS admitted, flag the intent explicitly in an appropriately-named `answer` field, and let your excerpts carry the strongest available evidence — direct proof-of-absence ("not listed", "n/a") if the page provides it; otherwise, try at least capturing the page segments where the missing info would plausibly have appeared if it existed, where applicable.

## `aerospace_authorized_distribution_reciprocity`

For 100+ aerospace or aviation-parts distributors, aftermarket suppliers, MRO-parts channels, or comparable public aviation-parts channels, find 3+ OEM/manufacturer counterparties per distributor where the distributor publicly carries or represents the OEM and the OEM independently acknowledges that distributor or channel; for each such (`distributor`, `oem`) pair and each of the 2 relationship sides (details on per-side specifics below), supply a relationship-substantiating source (i.e. 1+ URL).

The useful public provenance is reciprocal channel evidence, not a supplier ranking, due-diligence verdict, procurement recommendation, or part-fit decision.

The relationship sides of interest, which we refer to as `reference_type`, are:
- `carries`: a distributor-side statement naming `oem` as a carried, represented, distributed, supported, offered, or comparable OEM/product line in an aerospace, aviation-parts, avionics, engine, aircraft-systems, aftermarket, or MRO-parts channel.
- `authorizes`: an OEM-side statement naming `distributor` as an authorized distributor, appointed distributor, distribution partner, channel partner, representative, or comparable public channel.

There is no predefined list of eligible distributors or OEMs. `distributor` ought to be a real aerospace/aviation-parts distributor, aftermarket supplier, MRO-parts channel, or comparable public channel, and `oem` ought to be a real aviation/aerospace component, avionics, engine, aircraft-systems, parts, or equipment OEM/manufacturer. Same-company aliases, same-parent or same-corporate-family relationships, internal product-line or brand co-presence, airline/airport/service-only relationships, and unrelated generic industrial distribution do not qualify. The source ought to be a public, page-evaluable URL; closed apps, login-only pages, broken pages, and search snippets without page content do not qualify. The evidence sought is source-stated public channel evidence only, not a conclusion about current legal authorization, compliance, airworthiness, safety, product suitability, RFQ/stock/price availability, risk/sanctions status, contacts, or procurement fitness.

Requirements:
- The page should communicate that it is controlled by, or contains strong in-page ownership anchors for, the party being cited: for `carries`, that party is `distributor`; for `authorizes`, that party is `oem`. Official distributor/OEM domains, official partner locators, channel pages, line-card/product-line pages, official newsroom or appointment notices, product/integration pages, and official case/partner pages can be eligible surfaces; third-party listicles, marketplaces, RFQ pages, generic catalog pages, independent aggregators, press-wire republications not controlled by the cited side, FLIS/NSN mirror pages, part-number-only listings, stock/price/availability pages, and generic search pages typically will not communicate the necessary cited-party ownership.

Vague category references such as "leading avionics manufacturers" or "authorized aerospace distributors" do not count without the named counterparty.
- The page must acknowledge a concrete aerospace/aviation-parts channel relationship at the bar appropriate to `reference_type`: for `carries`, distributor-controlled line cards, OEM partner pages, distribution pages, product-line pages, or comparable distributor-side prose can pass when they state that the distributor carries, represents, distributes, supports, or offers the OEM/product line; for `authorizes`, OEM-controlled partner-locator entries, authorized distributor listings, appointment notices, distribution-agreement announcements, named channel-partner pages, or comparable OEM-controlled prose are required. Logo walls, brand co-presence, pages that merely name both parties, and generic "where to buy" scaffolds without a named channel relationship do not satisfy this requirement.
- The cited page evidence should keep any authorization language as source-stated public evidence for the channel relationship itself. Evidence that invites an independent conclusion about current legal validity, compliance, airworthiness, safety, product suitability, procurement fitness, sanctions/risk posture, contacts, RFQ workflow, stock, or pricing does not satisfy this task.
