## Universal rules

The following rules apply to every task below.

**Identifier discipline.** Same entity → same string. Different entities → different strings. When you're unsure whether two names refer to the same thing (spelling variants, model editions, product versions), don't offload the ambiguity to the user — decide and commit. Don't hedge by splitting "just in case" or merging "probably close enough." Both failures cost credit.

**More is (usually) better.** Whenever the task says "at least N" / "N+" / etc, going past N generally helps your score — treat those as soft floors, not exact targets.

**No duplicate entities.** Do not, however increase volume via duplicate entities, all the entities must be meaningfully different, entity-duplicating rows will generally be penalized; in particular, do not supply multiple rows per entity to "supply the answer in chunks", which will also be treated as entity duplication.

**Every `url` you submit must be fetchable.** Do not submit URLs you expect to be non-resolvable (DNS failure, dead host) as your `url`. Evidence should come from an available `url` (even if you wished to, say, provide evidence for some URL's unhealthiness).

**Every row carries `excerpts`** — verbatim or near-verbatim quotes from the source page (whitespace, punctuation, ellipses to skip irrelevant clauses are fine) **with semantics preserved**. An excerpt is what the page literally says, in the meaning the page intends. Fabrication, paraphrase that shifts meaning, sentence-stitching across sections, or selective cropping that flips a hedge into confidence — all fail.

The excerpts collectively make the answer evident. *Every* task-required claim / task-asked question / answer field / etc MUST have its support visible somewhere in the excerpt set — not just nearby on the page. The reader's test: imagine someone sees only your excerpts (with no access to the rest of the page); can they verify each piece of your answer? If a page genuinely doesn't carry what the task asks for, find a different page or skip the entity rather than fish for tangential excerpts. If you deem paraphrasing necessary / desirable for proper answer delivery, that's admirable and encouraged: paraphrase to your heart's desire within `answer` fields, make new `answer` fields and redistribute summaries among them as you see fit, but excerpts stay faithful and fully evidence-complete.

**Page contents only.** This is a task about citing web pages for human consumers, and citations are expected to be human-usable — both in where they are sourced from and in how well they stand on their own, out of page context. Excerpts come from the web-page main text — what a human reader sees on the page. Excerpts should also look sensible by themselves, with their information-bearing intent clear. API response blobs, page metadata fields (timestamps, view counts, score numbers), structured-data payloads (`__NEXT_DATA__`, JSON-LD, OpenGraph), and other “robot-side” sources / page representations are out of scope. In a similar vein, be wary of citing image captions / on-hover alt text / infoboxes / specially rendered bibliography or reference units / UI or navigation elements / etc. (unless confident in both their visibility and critical utility for the task), and avoid citing image contents, hyperlink-encoded URLs, and similar evidence surfaces altogether: anything outside the straightforward “main body of text” risks reducing citation ergonomics to the point where it is considered unusable.

**Signaling absence.** If you mean for a blank or sentinel `answer` field to assert "this required information isn't on the page" (vs. "I missed it"): first verify the task warrants such an option — many tasks treat blank-required as an invalid entity. When absence IS admitted, flag the intent explicitly in an appropriately-named `answer` field, and let your excerpts carry the strongest available evidence — direct proof-of-absence ("not listed", "n/a") if the page provides it; otherwise, try at least capturing the page segments where the missing info would plausibly have appeared if it existed, where applicable.

## `christian_radio_network_contacts`

For 50+ radio networks, cover the 3 contact channels listed below for each network by supplying a source (i.e. 1+ URL under each channel) which exposes a focused, usable contact detail clearly scoped to the channel in question; each network ought to be a United-States-operating Christian / religious radio network.

The aim is a sortable directory an audience could actually use to reach each network — office line, on-air request line, or a written inquiry address — so the value lives in details a listener could dial, mail, or email today.

Contact channels:
- `office_contact`: the network's corporate or headquarters reachability — a main office / business phone line, a mailing or physical address, and the network's own website identity. The bar reads like an organizational identity surface: an "About" / "Contact Us" / "Contact" page, a station/network footer or masthead, or a staff/business-office directory entry on the network's own site, where the office number and/or postal address are presented as the way to reach the organization itself.
- `request_line`: the network's listener-facing call line — a studio line, request line, on-air phone, prayer or comment line, or text-the-studio number meant for the audience rather than business callers. The bar reads like a listening / on-air / program-contact surface: a "Ways to Listen", "Contact the Studio", program page, or on-air call/text invitation that publishes a number the audience is invited to use during or about programming.
- `inquiry_email`: the network's written-contact address — a general inquiries, listener-comments, info, or department mailbox the public can write to. The bar reads like a contact / inquiry surface: a "Contact Us" form-or-email page, a footer/contact email, or a department mailbox on the network's own site, where a real, reachable email address (not a generic "subscribe" capture) is offered for getting in touch.

The sources should be fully public, accessible, and usable (e.g. not paywall-guarded, bare app pages, or login screens).

Requirements:
- The page must clearly identify the named radio network. A network keeps one identity across its legal / parent-organization name, its on-air brand, and descriptor or punctuation variants; but a separately-branded sibling network under the same parent is a different network, and an individual local station is not the same as the larger network it carries unless that station is itself the network's own primary public identity.
- The page must credibly present the network as a United-States-operating Christian / religious radio network — its own broadcast identity, faith-format framing, or US station/frequency footprint — not merely an unrelated organization that shares a similar name.
- The page must make visible the source role appropriate to the contact channel it is cited under, matching the channel's definition above, and it must be the network's own first-party identity — its own site, official station page, or an officially-operated social or listing page that visibly belongs to the network. A `request_line` source must carry listener-facing framing inviting the audience to phone or text in; a bare corporate switchboard published only as the way to reach the organization, with no such framing, must not be cited as a `request_line` source even when the same number would serve as an `office_contact`. A second-hand re-reporting source — a station-aggregator directory, a "radio stations near you" comparison site, a Wikipedia or news article quoting the network, or a generic business-listing scrape — does not satisfy the source role, even when it prints a correct-looking phone number, address, or email, however polished the page.
- The page should expose a focused contact detail clearly scoped to the named network and contact channel. For `office_contact`, this means a concrete office/business phone number, a mailing or physical address, or the network's own website identity presented as the way to reach the organization. For `request_line`, it means a concrete listener-facing phone or text number framed for the audience — a studio, request, on-air, prayer, or comment line. For `inquiry_email`, it means a concrete, reachable email address offered for public inquiries or listener contact.
