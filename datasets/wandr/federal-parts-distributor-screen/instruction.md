## Universal rules

The following rules apply to every task below.

**Identifier discipline.** Same entity → same string. Different entities → different strings. When you're unsure whether two names refer to the same thing (spelling variants, model editions, product versions), don't offload the ambiguity to the user — decide and commit. Don't hedge by splitting "just in case" or merging "probably close enough." Both failures cost credit.

**More is (usually) better.** Whenever the task says "at least N" / "N+" / etc, going past N generally helps your score — treat those as soft floors, not exact targets.

**No duplicate entities.** Do not, however increase volume via duplicate entities, all the entities must be meaningfully different, entity-duplicating rows will generally be penalized; in particular, do not supply multiple rows per entity to "supply the answer in chunks", which will also be treated as entity duplication.

**Every `url` you submit must be fetchable.** Do not submit URLs you expect to be non-resolvable (DNS failure, dead host) as your `url`. Evidence should come from an available `url` (even if you wished to, say, provide evidence for some URL's unhealthiness).

**Every row carries `excerpts`** — verbatim or near-verbatim quotes from the source page (whitespace, punctuation, ellipses to skip irrelevant clauses are fine) **with semantics preserved**. An excerpt is what the page literally says, in the meaning the page intends. Fabrication, paraphrase that shifts meaning, sentence-stitching across sections, or selective cropping that flips a hedge into confidence — all fail.

The excerpts collectively make the answer evident. *Every* task-required claim / task-asked question / answer field / etc MUST have its support visible somewhere in the excerpt set — not just nearby on the page. The reader's test: imagine someone sees only your excerpts (with no access to the rest of the page); can they verify each piece of your answer? If a page genuinely doesn't carry what the task asks for, find a different page or skip the entity rather than fish for tangential excerpts. If you deem paraphrasing necessary / desirable for proper answer delivery, that's admirable and encouraged: paraphrase to your heart's desire within `answer` fields, make new `answer` fields and redistribute summaries among them as you see fit, but excerpts stay faithful and fully evidence-complete.

**Page contents only.** This is a task about citing web pages for human consumers, and citations are expected to be human-usable — both in where they are sourced from and in how well they stand on their own, out of page context. Excerpts come from the web-page main text — what a human reader sees on the page. Excerpts should also look sensible by themselves, with their information-bearing intent clear. API response blobs, page metadata fields (timestamps, view counts, score numbers), structured-data payloads (`__NEXT_DATA__`, JSON-LD, OpenGraph), and other “robot-side” sources / page representations are out of scope. In a similar vein, be wary of citing image captions / on-hover alt text / infoboxes / specially rendered bibliography or reference units / UI or navigation elements / etc. (unless confident in both their visibility and critical utility for the task), and avoid citing image contents, hyperlink-encoded URLs, and similar evidence surfaces altogether: anything outside the straightforward “main body of text” risks reducing citation ergonomics to the point where it is considered unusable.

**Signaling absence.** If you mean for a blank or sentinel `answer` field to assert "this required information isn't on the page" (vs. "I missed it"): first verify the task warrants such an option — many tasks treat blank-required as an invalid entity. When absence IS admitted, flag the intent explicitly in an appropriately-named `answer` field, and let your excerpts carry the strongest available evidence — direct proof-of-absence ("not listed", "n/a") if the page provides it; otherwise, try at least capturing the page segments where the missing info would plausibly have appeared if it existed, where applicable.

## `federal_parts_distributor_screen`

For 40+ distributors, cover the 3 evidence facets listed below for each distributor by supplying a source (i.e. 1+ URL under each facet) that exposes a focused, tangible finding clearly scoped to the facet in question.

Each distributor is a real reseller or supplier operating in the fastener, hand-tool, or seal-and-gasket supply space. The goal is a sourceable supplier profile that separates a distributor's stated authorization and documentation posture, its fulfillment flexibility, and its federal-procurement footprint, so each can be weighed on its own evidence rather than blurred together.

Each source is a fully public, accessible, usable page — not a paywall-guarded page, a login screen, or a bare app page.

Evidence facets:
- `authorized_supply`: the distributor's standing as an authorized or franchised reseller for named brand-name or military-specification product lines, together with its stated ability to furnish material traceability and certificates of conformance.
- `flexible_fulfillment`: the distributor's stated capability to ship directly to a third party on the customer's behalf — drop-ship, blind-ship, or private-label / neutral-packaging fulfillment.
- `federal_award_history`: what a records check turns up on the distributor's federal direct-award activity, read off a procurement-award or government-entity-registry surface keyed to the distributor's own identity.

Requirements:
- The page must clearly identify the named distributor.
- The page must make its facet-appropriate source role visible. For `authorized_supply`, this means authorization and documentation language carried in the distributor's own line-card, brand-authorization, capabilities, or quality wording — cues such as "authorized distributor", "franchised distributor", "factory-authorized", a named manufacturer line card, "certificate of conformance" / "C of C", "material test report", "lot traceability", "mill certs", "DFARS", or a named distributor quality standard (e.g. AS9120, ISO 9001) — not a generic catalog listing of products for sale. For `flexible_fulfillment`, this means fulfillment-program wording on a shipping, services, capabilities, or program surface — cues such as "drop ship", "blind ship", "ships under your name", "neutral packing slip", or "private label" — not an inference from the mere existence of an online store. For `federal_award_history`, this means a procurement-award or government-entity-registry surface presenting the distributor's federal footprint through awardee-profile, contract-record, or entity-registry page roles — cues such as a recipient / awardee profile, a contract-award detail record, an entity-registration or commercial-and-government-entity (CAGE) listing, award totals or counts, awarding-agency and action-date columns, or contract / solicitation identifiers — not a self-published "we serve government customers" marketing claim. Across every facet, second-hand re-reporting — aggregators, comparison or supplier-directory stats sites, and news or blog posts quoting someone else's authorization, fulfillment, or award numbers — does not carry the facet-appropriate source role; the source must be the distributor's own capability / authorization wording or a procurement-award / government-entity-registry record.
- The page must expose a focused finding clearly scoped to the named distributor and evidence facet. For `authorized_supply`, this means a concrete authorization signal tied to named brands or specifications together with a stated traceability or certificate-of-conformance capability. For `flexible_fulfillment`, this means a concrete blind-ship, neutral-packaging, ships-under-the-customer's-name, or private-label capability the distributor states it offers; a stated drop-ship or generic custom-labeling capability on its own, without that blind / neutral-packaging / ships-under-the-customer's-name / private-label behavior, is not enough. For `federal_award_history`, this means a specific records-check result — named awards, totals, counts, agency, or dates tying the distributor to federal direct awards, or a credible no-result / not-found reading from such a surface — reported with the surface it was read from.
