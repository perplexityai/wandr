## Universal rules

The following rules apply to every task below.

**Identifier discipline.** Same entity → same string. Different entities → different strings. When you're unsure whether two names refer to the same thing (spelling variants, model editions, product versions), don't offload the ambiguity to the user — decide and commit. Don't hedge by splitting "just in case" or merging "probably close enough." Both failures cost credit.

**More is (usually) better.** Whenever the task says "at least N" / "N+" / etc, going past N generally helps your score — treat those as soft floors, not exact targets.

**No duplicate entities.** Do not, however increase volume via duplicate entities, all the entities must be meaningfully different, entity-duplicating rows will generally be penalized; in particular, do not supply multiple rows per entity to "supply the answer in chunks", which will also be treated as entity duplication.

**Every `url` you submit must be fetchable.** Do not submit URLs you expect to be non-resolvable (DNS failure, dead host) as your `url`. Evidence should come from an available `url` (even if you wished to, say, provide evidence for some URL's unhealthiness).

**Every row carries `excerpts`** — verbatim or near-verbatim quotes from the source page (whitespace, punctuation, ellipses to skip irrelevant clauses are fine) **with semantics preserved**. An excerpt is what the page literally says, in the meaning the page intends. Fabrication, paraphrase that shifts meaning, sentence-stitching across sections, or selective cropping that flips a hedge into confidence — all fail.

The excerpts collectively make the answer evident. *Every* task-required claim / task-asked question / answer field / etc MUST have its support visible somewhere in the excerpt set — not just nearby on the page. The reader's test: imagine someone sees only your excerpts (with no access to the rest of the page); can they verify each piece of your answer? If a page genuinely doesn't carry what the task asks for, find a different page or skip the entity rather than fish for tangential excerpts. If you deem paraphrasing necessary / desirable for proper answer delivery, that's admirable and encouraged: paraphrase to your heart's desire within `answer` fields, make new `answer` fields and redistribute summaries among them as you see fit, but excerpts stay faithful and fully evidence-complete.

**Page contents only.** This is a task about citing web pages for human consumers, and citations are expected to be human-usable — both in where they are sourced from and in how well they stand on their own, out of page context. Excerpts come from the web-page main text — what a human reader sees on the page. Excerpts should also look sensible by themselves, with their information-bearing intent clear. API response blobs, page metadata fields (timestamps, view counts, score numbers), structured-data payloads (`__NEXT_DATA__`, JSON-LD, OpenGraph), and other “robot-side” sources / page representations are out of scope. In a similar vein, be wary of citing image captions / on-hover alt text / infoboxes / specially rendered bibliography or reference units / UI or navigation elements / etc. (unless confident in both their visibility and critical utility for the task), and avoid citing image contents, hyperlink-encoded URLs, and similar evidence surfaces altogether: anything outside the straightforward “main body of text” risks reducing citation ergonomics to the point where it is considered unusable.

**Signaling absence.** If you mean for a blank or sentinel `answer` field to assert "this required information isn't on the page" (vs. "I missed it"): first verify the task warrants such an option — many tasks treat blank-required as an invalid entity. When absence IS admitted, flag the intent explicitly in an appropriately-named `answer` field, and let your excerpts carry the strongest available evidence — direct proof-of-absence ("not listed", "n/a") if the page provides it; otherwise, try at least capturing the page segments where the missing info would plausibly have appeared if it existed, where applicable.

## `india_marketplace_seller_terms`

For 150+ online marketplaces or marketplace-like seller/supplier channels operating in India, cover each of the 4 seller-facing evidence facets listed below by supplying a public source (i.e. 1+ URL under each facet) that identifies the marketplace/channel and supports a concrete public seller-facing fact for that facet.

The evidence is neutral public provenance for seller-facing marketplace surfaces, not a marketplace ranking, seller recommendation, fee-optimization table, procurement guide, legal/compliance guide, traffic-performance comparison, outreach list, or contact-enrichment surface.

Seller-facing evidence facets:
- `seller_access`: seller, supplier, merchant, brand, partner, or service-provider onboarding/access surface; registration path; seller portal; eligibility, documentation, or account-creation pathway.
- `seller_economic_terms`: public seller cost, commission, referral fee, closing fee, shipping/fulfillment fee, plan/subscription price, free-registration statement, zero-commission statement, no-hidden-charge statement, or page-stated negotiated/deferred public-rate posture.
- `seller_policy_or_governance`: seller agreement, seller terms, payment/settlement policy, returns policy, restricted/prohibited categories, listing/catalog governance, seller conduct, network policy, or comparable seller-applicable rule surface with operative obligations, restrictions, rights, or processes.
- `category_or_program_scope`: source-stated marketplace categories, B2B/B2C or vertical scope, seller/supplier program boundaries, network/app role, channel coverage, India program/geographic scope, or other seller-program boundary.

Marketplace/channel distinctness follows the public seller-program identity. Seller portals, supplier portals, merchant/vendor/host/provider role labels, onboarding surface names, domain variants, India-label variants, and legal-operator names are the same marketplace/channel when they point to the same public seller, supplier, merchant, host, or provider program: for example, Amazon India, Amazon.in, Sell on Amazon India, and Amazon.in Seller all name the Amazon.in seller channel when the source is the same Amazon.in seller program. Independently branded marketplace channels, sub-brands, ONDC participant apps, and separately branded seller/supplier/provider programs count separately only when public evidence presents a distinct channel identity and distinct seller/supplier/provider path.

The marketplace/channel ought to be a real online marketplace or marketplace-like digital-commerce channel with a public pathway for third-party sellers, suppliers, merchants, brands, service providers, seller-network participants, or comparable commercial partners to offer goods or services through the channel. Pure first-party retail, brand-store, franchise-recruiting, driver/operator onboarding, affiliate, reseller, agency, or lead-generation pages do not count unless the page establishes a third-party seller/supplier/provider marketplace role. ONDC itself is not an ordinary marketplace, but ONDC participant apps/channels can count when the page establishes a seller/supplier marketplace role.

The sources should be public, accessible, and usable as normal pages; login-only screens, broken pages, empty app shells, generic redirects, search-result pages, and consumer-only pages with no seller-facing marketplace content are not enough.

Requirements:
- The page must clearly identify the named marketplace or seller channel.
- The page must tie the marketplace, seller channel, or seller-facing program to India operations, India-facing sellers/suppliers/brands/partners, or an India-market digital-commerce network.
- The page should make official/operator control or direct marketplace affiliation visible through a facet-specific page role. For `seller_access`, the source should read as an onboarding, registration, seller-portal, eligibility, documentation, or account-access surface. For `seller_economic_terms`, it should read as a pricing, fee, commission, rate-card, plan, commercial-terms, or clearly named fee/pricing section. For `seller_policy_or_governance`, it should read as seller terms, agreement, policy, rules, payment/settlement, returns, listing/catalog, restricted-category, conduct, or network-governance material. For `category_or_program_scope`, it should read as a category, vertical, program-scope, service-coverage, network-role, or seller/supplier-program boundary surface. Broad seller homepages, FAQs, partner pages, terms pages, PDFs, or help centers are sufficient only for the facet whose page role and load-bearing section they visibly match.
- The page must expose concrete seller-facing evidence for the selected facet. For `seller_access`, this means an actionable onboarding, registration, eligibility, required-document, seller-portal, partner-path, or access-path fact. For `seller_economic_terms`, this means a public cost/rate/fee/plan/free-registration/zero-commission/no-hidden-charge/negotiated-or-deferred-rate posture stated by the page; a missing public fee source is not itself an eligible claim unless the page states the relevant negative or deferred-rate posture. For `seller_policy_or_governance`, this means an operative seller-applicable agreement, term, payment, settlement, returns, restricted-category, listing/catalog, conduct, network-policy, obligation, restriction, right, or process. For `category_or_program_scope`, this means a source-stated category, vertical, B2B/B2C, seller-program, network/app-role, channel-coverage, India-scope, or seller-program boundary fact. Generic seller availability, marketplace existence, or all-purpose partner language by itself is not enough.
